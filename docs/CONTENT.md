# Texas Interactive Map App — Content (Source of Truth)

Approved content for the interactive Texas map app. Two modes, toggled by dropdown: **Geographic Regions of Texas** and **Native People of Texas**. This file is the single source of truth for app copy — pull directly from it when building the data files in Phase 1 of [BUILD_PLAN.md](./BUILD_PLAN.md).

## Reading level
All on-map text reads at a 5th-grade level (short sentences, everyday words) while keeping exact 7th-grade TEKS vocabulary (escarpment, subtropical, sedentary, etc.). The first time a hard word appears in a region's write-up, a short, simple definition follows it in parentheses. The bullets below are raw content — convert them into short paragraphs in that simplified style when writing the data files.

## 1. MODE A: Geographic Regions of Texas

Four regions (TEKS §113.19(c)(8)(A)). Each has: subregions + their cities, climate, plants and animals, resources, and jobs, plus landforms/rivers for map drawing.

### Coastal Plains (green)
- **Subregions & cities:** Gulf Coastal Plain (the flat land right along the coast, with wetlands (soggy, low land like swamps) and barrier islands (long, narrow sand islands that protect the coast from waves)) — Corpus Christi, Galveston, Brownsville, Laredo; Piney Woods (thick pine and hardwood forest in East Texas) — Tyler, Beaumont; Post Oak Belt (a strip of scattered trees and grassy openings between the forest and the prairie) — near Bryan; Blackland Prairie (a strip of rich, dark, clay-like soil good for farming, running from the Red River down to San Antonio) — Dallas, Waco, San Antonio, Austin (eastern edge)
- **Major cities overall:** Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, Austin (edge), Corpus Christi, Galveston, Brownsville, Laredo, Tyler, Beaumont
- **Landforms/water:** Gulf of Mexico (forms the east/south edge), Balcones Escarpment (a long, steep cliff or slope where the land suddenly rises — forms the west edge), Galveston Island, bays, estuaries (places where a river meets the ocean and fresh and salt water mix), and barrier islands; Rivers: Trinity, Brazos, Sabine, Neches, Rio Grande, Nueces, Colorado (near its mouth, where it meets the Gulf)
- **Climate:** Subtropical (warm and rainy most of the year, close to a tropical climate) and humid — hot, sticky summers often over 90°F; mild winters (it rarely freezes near the coast); rainy, stormy springs; hurricane risk in the fall
- **Plants:** Pine, oak, and hickory trees, plus cypress trees in the swamps; salt-tolerant grasses along the coast, bluestem grass in the prairies; cattails, wildflowers
- **Animals:** White-tailed deer, raccoons, opossums, armadillos; great blue herons, egrets, pelicans, and many migrating birds; alligators, turtles, snakes
- **Resources:** Oil, natural gas, timber (wood from trees), rich farm soil, seafood, deep-water ports (harbors deep enough for large ships)
- **Jobs:** Oil and gas engineering, shipping and port work, commercial fishing, farming (cotton, rice, citrus fruit), aerospace work (Houston, home of NASA), the lumber industry

### North Central Plains (yellow)
- **Subregions & cities:** Grand Prairie (rolling, grassy land with limestone (a soft white/gray rock) just under the soil) — near Fort Worth; Cross Timbers (two narrow strips of rugged, sandy-soiled oak forest that run north to south); Rolling Plains (the largest subregion here, with rolling hills and grassland, in the western part) — Abilene, Wichita Falls
- **Major cities:** Fort Worth, Abilene, Wichita Falls, Waco
- **Landforms/water:** Balcones Escarpment (east edge), Caprock Escarpment (a steep cliff marking where the High Plains suddenly rise up — west edge), Red River (the border with Oklahoma); Rivers: Brazos
- **Climate:** Continental (a climate with big temperature swings between seasons) — stormy springs with tornado risk; hot, dry summers over 90°F; cold winters with occasional snow and ice
- **Plants:** Bluestem and indiangrass (native prairie grasses); live oak, post oak, and mesquite trees; prickly pear cactus, wildflowers
- **Animals:** White-tailed deer, coyotes, bobcats, prairie dogs; bobwhite quail, wild turkey, hawks and eagles; rattlesnakes, lizards
- **Resources:** Fertile (rich, good-for-growing-crops) soil, grasslands, oil, natural gas, coal
- **Jobs:** Cattle ranching, dairy farming, factory work, aerospace and defense work (Fort Worth), farming (corn, peanuts, wheat, cotton)

### Great Plains (orange)
- **Subregions & cities:** High Plains, also called the Llano Estacado (Spanish for "Staked Plains" — a huge, flat, high stretch of land in the Panhandle) — Amarillo, Lubbock, Midland, Odessa; Edwards Plateau (a raised, flat area of land), known as the Texas Hill Country (limestone hills, canyons, and natural springs) — San Angelo, Del Rio, Fredericksburg, Austin (eastern edge)
- **Landforms/water:** Caprock Escarpment (east edge of the High Plains), Palo Duro Canyon (a deep canyon carved by wind and water), Llano Uplift (a dome of ancient rock pushed up from below), Balcones Escarpment (edge of the Edwards Plateau); Rivers: Colorado, Brazos (both start here), Canadian, Pecos
- **Climate:** Semi-arid (fairly dry, with low rainfall, but not as dry as a desert) — very hot, dry summers over 100°F; strong winds and tornado risk in the Panhandle in spring; cold winters with blizzards, sometimes called "blue northers" (fast-moving cold storms)
- **Plants:** Short grasses like buffalo grass and blue grama; juniper trees, scattered mesquite; sagebrush and other plants that need little water
- **Animals:** Pronghorn (a fast, deer-like animal), coyotes, jackrabbits, prairie dogs; scaled quail, hawks and other birds of prey; rattlesnakes, horned lizards
- **Resources:** The Ogallala Aquifer (a huge underground layer of rock that holds water), oil and gas, wind energy, grassland for livestock
- **Jobs:** Farming (both dryland and irrigated, meaning watered by canals or sprinklers), wind turbine technician work, oil field work, sheep and goat ranching, tourism in the Hill Country

### Mountains and Basins (red — also called the Trans-Pecos, meaning "across the Pecos River")
- **Cities:** El Paso, Fort Davis
- **Landforms:** Guadalupe Mountains (home to Guadalupe Peak, 8,749 feet tall — the highest point in Texas), Davis Mountains, Chisos Mountains, the Rio Grande (the river that forms the border with Mexico and has carved deep canyons here), desert basins (low, bowl-shaped areas of land between mountain ranges); Big Bend National Park, Guadalupe Mountains National Park, Fort Davis National Historic Site; Rivers: Rio Grande, Pecos
- **Climate:** Arid (very dry) and desert-like — hot days and cool nights in summer; mild days and cold nights in winter; it can even snow in the mountains
- **Plants:** Cacti (prickly pear, cholla), creosote bush, mesquite, yucca, agave
- **Animals:** Mule deer, mountain lions, javelinas (wild, pig-like animals), desert cottontail rabbits; roadrunners, hummingbirds, desert sparrows; rattlesnakes, Gila monsters, lizards
- **Resources:** Minerals like limestone, talc, and gypsum, plus beautiful desert and mountain scenery
- **Jobs:** Mining, tourism, trade and shipping (El Paso is one of the busiest land ports — places where goods cross the border by truck or train — in the whole country), a small amount of ranching

**Master major-cities list (shown on BOTH maps):** Houston, San Antonio, Dallas, Fort Worth, Austin, El Paso, Corpus Christi, Lubbock, Amarillo, Waco, Abilene, Wichita Falls, Midland, Odessa, Laredo, Brownsville, Galveston, Del Rio, San Angelo, Beaumont, Tyler

## 2. MODE B: Native People of Texas

**Important nuance:** these four *cultural* regions do NOT line up 1:1 with the four *geographic* regions above (e.g., Gulf and Southeastern cultures both sit within the Coastal Plains geography; Plains culture spans North Central Plains + Great Plains; Puebloan ≈ Mountains and Basins). The shading/shapes on this map will look different from Mode A's. This matches TEKS §113.19(c)(2)(A) exactly ("Gulf, Plains, Puebloan, and Southeastern").

### Gulf Culture (south/coastal Texas)
- **Tribes:** Karankawa, Coahuiltecan (also called Carrizo)
- **Lifestyle:** Nomadic (moving from place to place instead of staying put) hunter-gatherers; small family groups that moved often along the coast
- **Housing:** Portable wigwams — dome-shaped huts made from bent poles covered in animal skins or woven mats (Karankawa); bent-branch huts (Coahuiltecan)
- **Primary food sources:** Fish, shellfish, turtles, alligators, mesquite pods, cactus fruit, roots, berries
- **Key cultural elements:** Rubbed alligator grease on their skin to keep bugs away; held *mitotes* — ceremonies with dancing and celebration

### Plains Culture (North Central Plains / Great Plains)
- **Tribes:** Comanche, Apache (Kiowa, Wichita, and Tonkawa also lived in this region)
- **Lifestyle:** Highly nomadic and equestrian (skilled horseback riders); their whole way of life centered on the buffalo; once the Spanish brought horses to Texas, they became powerful warriors
- **Housing:** Portable teepees made from buffalo hide stretched over wooden poles
- **Primary food sources:** Buffalo (meat and pemmican — dried meat mixed with fat and berries, made to last a long time), deer, wild plants
- **Key cultural elements:** Became expert horse riders for hunting and fighting; the Apache also used seasonal farming camps called *rancherías*; the Wichita were known for tattoos around their eyes, sometimes called "raccoon eyes"

### Puebloan Culture (Mountains and Basins)
- **Tribes:** Jumano (Tigua and other Pueblo groups also lived here)
- **Lifestyle:** Sedentary (staying in one place instead of moving around) farmers and traders who lived in permanent villages near rivers
- **Housing:** Permanent, multi-story pueblos (buildings) made of adobe — bricks made from dried mud and straw
- **Primary food sources:** Corn, beans, and squash that they farmed; they also traded for buffalo meat and hides
- **Key cultural elements:** Wore *rayado* — striped face markings; acted as go-betweens, trading goods between the Plains tribes and the Pueblo tribes; the Tigua were known for their pottery

### Southeastern Culture (Piney Woods / East Texas)
- **Tribes:** Caddo (Tawakoni and Kitsai were also part of the larger Hasinai group — a confederacy, meaning several tribes joined together)
- **Lifestyle:** Sedentary farmers with a well-organized society led by a chief; women held important roles in the community
- **Housing:** Permanent, dome-shaped grass houses — a frame of wooden poles covered in woven grass
- **Primary food sources:** Corn (their main crop), beans, pumpkins, and squash; they also hunted deer
- **Key cultural elements:** Skilled potters with an organized, complex society; they greeted European explorers with the word "*Tejas*," meaning "friends" — this is where the name "Texas" comes from

## 4. Interaction Spec
- Dropdown toggle switches the whole map between Mode A and Mode B (different shapes/colors/data).
- Regions are shaded distinct colors per mode.
- Clicking a region: that region visually brightens, lifts (subtle shadow/elevation), and scales up slightly relative to the rest of the map; the other regions dim/recede.
- Info panel beside the map updates to show that region's full write-up in paragraph form — bullets above get converted into short paragraphs per region per mode.
- Mode A only: subregion boundaries are outlined within each region on the map.
- Major cities are marked/labeled on the map in both modes.
- Clicking a new region replaces the previously active one (only one active at a time); clicking the active region again returns to the default overview state.

## 5. Confirmed Build Decisions
- **Map fidelity:** Stylized regions — hand-traced, simplified shapes based on the reference maps provided (not GIS-accurate).
- **Wix integration:** Externally hosted static site, embedded into the Wix page via an iframe pointing at that hosted URL.
- **Scope:** Exactly the two modes described — no historic-landmarks layer or other TEKS enrichment.
